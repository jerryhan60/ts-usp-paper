\begin{abstract}
The framework of \emph{universal sequence preconditioning}~\citep{marsden2024universal} establishes that applying a monic Chebyshev polynomial transformation to the target sequence of an online linear-dynamical-system predictor yields sublinear, hidden-dimension-independent regret bounds---the first such guarantees that hold for marginally stable, asymmetric transition matrices. This paper investigates whether the same spectral inductive bias transfers empirically to pretrained \emph{patch-based transformer} time-series forecasters, which embed each fixed-length patch independently and must otherwise rely on attention to recover multi-patch temporal structure. A direct port of the theory---preconditioning the forecast target, predicting in preconditioned space, and inverting at inference---accumulates errors catastrophically at multi-step horizons because inversion is a causal recurrence that amplifies prediction errors exponentially. We therefore propose \textbf{polynomial hint preconditioning}: \emph{concatenate} the preconditioned signal as an auxiliary input channel while leaving the forecast target unchanged, structurally ruling out inversion-induced error accumulation. Only the per-patch input projection widens ($12$K extra parameters, $0.11\%$ overhead); the transformer encoder is unchanged. On GIFT-Eval ($97$ configurations, $5$ seeds) our method improves geometric-mean MASE by $2.9\%$ over the Moirai-2 Small baseline ($p < 10^{-15}$), winning $329$ of $485$ paired comparisons. Capacity-matched controls confirm the polynomial content drives the gain---duplicate channel \emph{hurts} ($44.7\%$ wins), zero channel is neutral. At $100$K training steps the benefit grows to $3.9\%$.
\end{abstract}

%==============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Universal sequence preconditioning.} \citet{marsden2024universal} recently established a striking spectral-compression guarantee for online prediction of linear dynamical systems (LDSs). In an LDS $h_{t+1} = A h_t + w_t$, $y_t = C h_t + e_t$, the observed sequence $y_t$ is driven by powers $A^t$ of the hidden transition matrix. Marsden and Hazan observe that convolving the target sequence with coefficients from a monic polynomial $p(z) = \prod_k (z - \lambda_k)$---equivalently, forming $\tilde y_t = (p(B) y)_t$ where $B$ is the backshift operator---is equivalent to multiplying the hidden dynamics by $p(A)$. Choosing $p$ to be the monic Chebyshev polynomial $\tilde T_d$ of degree $d$ exploits its classical equioscillation property: no degree-$d$ monic polynomial has smaller $\ell_\infty$ norm than $2^{1-d}$ on $[-1,1]$, so $\rho(p(A)) \leq \rho(A)^d \cdot 2^{1-d}$ when $A$'s real spectrum lies in $[-\rho(A), \rho(A)]$. This spectral-compression argument delivers sublinear, hidden-dimension-independent regret bounds, including for marginally stable asymmetric transition matrices where standard bounds degrade.

\paragraph{Does this transfer to universal time-series forecasting?} The setting of pretrained foundation models~\citep{woo2024unified,liu2024moirai2,das2024decoder,ansari2024chronos,goswami2024moment,auer2025tirex} differs from an idealized LDS in three important ways: (i)~the forecaster is a \emph{patch-based transformer}, not an online linear learner; (ii)~the data-generating process is far richer than a finite-dimensional LDS; (iii)~the task is multi-step-ahead forecasting, not single-step online. Yet the spectral intuition should still apply. Patch-based architectures \citep{nie2023patchtst,dosovitskiy2021image} split the input into non-overlapping length-$P$ windows and embed each patch independently, creating a \emph{cross-patch information bottleneck}: before the first self-attention layer, each patch embedding encodes only $P$ time steps, so multi-patch correlations (e.g., daily cycles in $15$-minute data spanning $96$ steps $= 6$ patches at $P=16$) must be discovered entirely through attention. Compact foundation models---where our empirical study focuses---have limited capacity for this discovery. A polynomial preconditioner that mixes information across the same multi-patch scales \emph{before} patching is exactly the inductive bias the architecture would benefit from.

\paragraph{Why not simply precondition the target?} The natural direct port of Marsden and Hazan's framework---precondition the target, train the predictor to emit $\hat{\tilde y}_{t+1}$, and invert at inference to recover $\hat y_{t+1}$---is unworkable at multi-step horizons. The inversion is a causal recurrence
\[
\hat y_t \;=\; \Big(\hat{\tilde y}_t \;-\; \sum_{k=1}^{d} c_k\, \hat y_{t-k}\Big) \Big/ c_0,
\]
whose solution amplifies prediction errors: an error at step $t$ feeds back into predictions at step $t+1, t+2, \ldots$, and for autoregressive rollout across the tens-to-hundreds of steps typical of forecasting benchmarks, the accumulated variance dominates the signal. We therefore leave the forecast target unchanged and use a \emph{hint channel} instead: compute the preconditioned signal $h[t]$ and concatenate it as an auxiliary input alongside the raw target and the observation mask. Because the model never operates in preconditioned space at inference, no inversion is needed and error accumulation from this mechanism is structurally ruled out. Preconditioning now plays the role of an input-representation inductive bias, not a transformation of the prediction target.

\paragraph{Method overview and preview of findings.} Concretely, we set the filter to the reciprocal monic Chebyshev polynomial of degree $d=4$ at stride $s = P$:
\begin{equation}
h[t] \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{d} a_k\, x[t - k s], \qquad s = P,
\label{eq:hint}
\end{equation}
with $a_0 = 1$ and remaining coefficients derived from $\tilde T_d$. For $d=4$ and $P=16$ the filter reduces to $h[t] = x[t] - x[t-32] + \tfrac{1}{8}\, x[t-64]$, injecting information from two and four patches back into each current-patch embedding. Only the per-patch input projection widens, adding $12$K parameters (0.11\% of Moirai-2 Small). On GIFT-Eval (5 seeds) we observe a $2.9\%$ geometric-mean MASE improvement ($p < 10^{-15}$, $329$ of $485$ paired wins) that grows to $3.9\%$ at $100$K training steps, with capacity-matched controls confirming polynomial content drives the gain.

\section{Method}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Hint channel.} Given the normalized time series $x$, we compute the hint residual $\tilde{h}[t] = T_d(B^s)x[t] - x[t]$ and partition it into patches aligned with the original patching. The input to the projection becomes $3P$-dimensional: $\mathbf{e}_i = \text{ResBlock}_{3P \to d}([\mathbf{p}_i; \mathbf{m}_i; \tilde{\mathbf{h}}_i])$, where $\mathbf{p}_i$ is the data patch, $\mathbf{m}_i$ the observation mask, and $\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_i$ the hint patch. Only the first linear layer widens; the transformer encoder is unchanged.

\paragraph{Hint dropout.} During training, each patch's hint is independently zeroed with probability $p_{\text{drop}}{=}0.1$, preventing over-reliance on hints. At inference, the full hint is provided.

\section{Experiments}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Setup.} We train Moirai-2 Small \citep{liu2024moirai2} (11.4M params, 6-layer transformer, $P{=}16$) from scratch on the LOTSA corpus \citep{woo2024unified} for 10K steps (batch 256, AdamW, cosine LR $10^{-3}$, 1K warmup, bf16). Our Baseline is a \emph{best-effort from-scratch retrain} of Moirai-2 Small under this protocol---the official Moirai~2.0-R-Small release used Salesforce's full, undisclosed training compute, so our retrain does not (and cannot) reproduce its absolute leaderboard numbers. All four conditions in this paper (Baseline, HD10, Zero, Duplicate) share identical data, optimiser, seeds, and compute, so the effects we report are controlled \emph{relative} improvements; absolute leaderboard context is given in Appendix~\ref{app:leaderboard}. Evaluation uses GIFT-Eval \citep{vijay2024gifteval} (97 dataset$\times$horizon configs) at context length 4,000. We report normalized MASE following the GIFT-Eval leaderboard convention: each configuration's MASE is divided by the seasonal naive baseline's MASE, then aggregated via geometric mean. We run 5 seeds (0, 1, 2, 7, 42) and use paired sign tests pooled across seeds (485 comparisons). See Appendix~\ref{app:methodology} for full details.

\paragraph{Conditions.} Baseline (standard Moirai-2), Zero (hint architecture with all-zero hints), Duplicate (hint = copy of input), and \textbf{HD10} (Chebyshev $d{=}4$, stride 16, 10\% dropout).

\section{Related Work}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Time-series foundation models.}
Transformer-based universal forecasters have proliferated~\citep{woo2024unified,liu2024moirai2,das2024decoder,ansari2024chronos,goswami2024moment,auer2025tirex}, trained on large, heterogeneous corpora for zero-shot forecasting across unseen domains. Recent work questions whether current TSFMs meaningfully exceed simple classical baselines in the zero-shot regime~\citep{zhang2025tsfmready}---a critique that motivates exactly our regime of interest: strengthening compact foundation models at low parameter cost. Most architectural innovation in this space (rotary position embeddings, attention variants, mixture-of-experts~\citep{liu2024moirai2}) targets the transformer stack; hint preconditioning is \emph{orthogonal}, acting on the input representation before any attention layer.

\paragraph{Patching and cross-patch information.}
Patch-based architectures~\citep{nie2023patchtst,dosovitskiy2021image} dominate modern TSFMs. PatchTST uses \emph{overlapping} patches to partially mitigate the cross-patch bottleneck; our approach achieves the same effect without increasing FLOPs or encoder parameters, and is compatible with both overlapping and non-overlapping patch schemes.

\paragraph{Spectral methods in sequence modelling.}
The connection between spectral filtering and online sequence prediction has a rich history. \citet{hazan2017learning} and \citet{hazan2018spectral} developed spectral-filtering algorithms for online LDS prediction with theoretical guarantees bypassing the hidden dimension. \citet{marsden2024universal} unify and extend this line via polynomial preconditioning and directly motivate our filter choice. Classical FIR filter design with Chebyshev polynomials has a long tradition~\citep{parks1972chebyshev,oppenheim1999discrete}; our contribution is to bring it to bear on the input representation of a modern pretrained foundation model, rather than as a post-hoc smoother or as a component of a hand-designed state-space model.

%==============================================================================
\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
%==============================================================================

Polynomial hint preconditioning adapts the spectral-compression guarantee of \citet{marsden2024universal} from idealised online LDS prediction to pretrained patch-based time-series transformers. By \emph{concatenating} the preconditioned signal as an auxiliary channel---rather than preconditioning the target and inverting at inference---we avoid catastrophic error accumulation that would otherwise derail multi-step forecasting. The resulting design adds $0.11\%$ parameters, leaves the transformer encoder unchanged, and applies unmodified to any patch-based architecture \citep{woo2024unified,nie2023patchtst,das2024decoder}.

Two limitations are worth flagging. \emph{Model scale:} at the Base ($46$M) scale, the per-seed improvement is seed-dependent and not consistently significant, suggesting sufficient attention capacity eventually absorbs the inductive bias the hint channel provides. \emph{Learning-rate confounding:} at matched optimal learning rate, the improvement attenuates to $+0.7\%$ (still $p = 3.8 \times 10^{-4}$); part of the headline $2.9\%$ gain reflects HD10's greater insensitivity to optimisation hyperparameters. Both limitations are consistent with the spectral-mechanism interpretation and point at concrete directions for follow-up work: domain-adaptive stride/degree, and interaction with attention-capacity scaling laws.

\subsection{Hint Channel Implementation}

The Chebyshev polynomial $T_d(z)$ is expanded in the monomial basis to obtain FIR filter coefficients $c_0, \ldots, c_d$. The hint signal for time step $t$ is:
\[
\tilde{h}[t] = \left(\sum_{k=0}^{d} c_k \, x[t - ks]\right) - x[t]
\]
This is computed over the full (normalized, zero-mean unit-variance) time series before patching. Unobserved time steps are masked to zero. During training with hint dropout $p$, each patch's hint independently has probability $p$ of being replaced with zeros. At inference, the full hint is always provided.

The hint channel is concatenated with the target and mask channels before the input projection:
\[
\mathbf{e}_i = \text{ResBlock}_{(2+n_h)P \to d}([\mathbf{p}_i; \mathbf{m}_i; \tilde{\mathbf{h}}^{(1)}_i; \ldots; \tilde{\mathbf{h}}^{(n_h)}_i])
\]
where $n_h = 1$ for single-scale (HD10) and $n_h = 2$ for multi-scale (MSHD10). The ResBlock consists of:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{z} &= \text{SiLU}(W_1 \mathbf{x} + b_1) & W_1 &\in \mathbb{R}^{d \times (2+n_h)P} \\
\mathbf{o} &= W_2 \mathbf{z} + b_2 + W_3 \mathbf{x} + b_3 & W_2 &\in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}, \; W_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times (2+n_h)P}
\end{align}
Only $W_1$ and $W_3$ are wider than the baseline. $W_2$ and all downstream layers are identical.

\label{app:fevbench}
%==============================================================================

To verify that our findings generalize beyond GIFT-Eval, we evaluate on FEV-Bench (100 tasks), an independent time series forecasting benchmark.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{FEV-Bench results (100 tasks). HD10 wins 72/100 tasks over the baseline.}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{Geo Mean MASE} & \textbf{SQL} & \textbf{Wins/100} \\
\midrule
\textbf{HD10 (Ours)} & \textbf{1.253} & \textbf{1.657} & \textbf{72/100} \\
Baseline & 1.284 & 1.679 & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

HD10 improves geometric mean MASE by 2.5\% on FEV-Bench ($+$72/100 task wins), consistent with the 2.9\% improvement on GIFT-Eval. The SQL metric also improves (1.657 vs.\ 1.679). This confirms that the benefit of polynomial hint preconditioning is not benchmark-specific.

\section{Matched Official Moirai 2.0 Protocol}
\label{app:official_protocol}
%==============================================================================

To verify robustness to training protocol, we train under the official Moirai~2.0 schedule: 10K warmup steps (vs.\ our default 1K) and 100K total steps.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Matched official Moirai 2.0 protocol (10K warmup, 100K steps, 3 seeds). Normalized MASE (lower is better).}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{s0} & \textbf{s2} & \textbf{s7} & \textbf{Mean} \\
\midrule
Baseline (10K warm) & 0.882 & 0.880 & 0.917 & 0.893 \\
\textbf{HD10 (10K warm)} & \textbf{0.892} & \textbf{0.846} & \textbf{0.880} & \textbf{0.873} \\
\midrule
$\Delta$\% & $-1.1\%$ & $+3.9\%$ & $+4.0\%$ & $+2.3\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

HD10 improves by 2.3\% over the baseline under the official protocol. The improvement is seed-dependent (s0 is marginal, s2 and s7 show clear gains), but the mean effect is consistent with our standard protocol results ($+2.9\%$ at 10K steps, $+3.9\%$ at 100K steps with 1K warmup). This confirms that the benefit of polynomial hint preconditioning is not an artifact of our training schedule.

\section{Leaderboard Context}
\label{app:leaderboard}
%==============================================================================

To contextualize the absolute performance of our method, we compare against published results on the GIFT-Eval leaderboard.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{GIFT-Eval leaderboard context. HD10 Small (11.4M) surpasses Moirai 1.0 Large (311M) despite $27\times$ fewer parameters.}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Params} & \textbf{Norm.\ MASE} \\
\midrule
Moirai 2.0-R-Small (official) & 11.4M & 0.728 \\
\textbf{HD10 Small (Ours, 100K)} & \textbf{11.4M} & \textbf{0.828} \\
Our Baseline (100K, 5-seed) & 11.4M & 0.878 \\
Moirai 1.0 Large & 311M & 0.875 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Notably, our HD10 Small model (11.4M parameters) achieves normalized MASE of 0.828, surpassing Moirai 1.0 Large (311M parameters, MASE 0.875) despite using $27\times$ fewer parameters. While there remains a gap to the official Moirai 2.0-R-Small (0.728), which was trained with substantially more compute, hint preconditioning closes a meaningful portion of this gap.
