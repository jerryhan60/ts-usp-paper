\begin{abstract}
Universal Sequence Preconditioning (USP) is a recent theoretical framework for online sequence prediction that preprocesses observations via a fixed polynomial convolution, yielding hidden-dimension-free sublinear regret for marginally stable linear dynamical systems. This paper investigates whether these techniques transfer empirically to zero-shot patch-based transformer time-series forecasters such as Moirai 2.0. We propose \textbf{polynomial input preconditioning}, where we concatenate the preconditioned signal as an auxiliary input channel while leaving the forecast target unchanged. By only adding $0.11\%$ parameters, our method improves a Moirai 2.0 Small baseline by $2.9\%$ geometric-mean MASE on GIFT-Eval and achieves similar gains on FEV-Bench. At a 100K-step budget, the method remains substantially better than the matched baseline, with a larger relative gap and lower cross-seed variance. Polynomial input preconditioning performs better with longer prediction horizon lengths, attaining a $5.3\%$ improvement on long horizon prediction tasks. Capacity-matched zero and duplicate-channel controls show that the gain comes from the polynomial content rather than extra parameters. These results suggest that time-series foundation models can benefit substantially from theory-guided structure injected before tokenization, offering a lightweight alternative to improving model performance without scaling model size or attention complexity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{Universal sequence preconditioning.} In 2025, based on the theory of linear dynamical systems, \citet{marsden2024universal} proposed Universal Sequence Preconditioning (USP) for online learning. The technique generalizes differencing; in particular, they suggest preprocessing the inputs via a fixed convolution of polynomial coefficients,
\[
x_t^{\mathrm{pre}} \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{d} c_k\,x_{t-k},
\]
where $c_k$ are fixed coefficients and $d$ is the polynomial degree. They prove that this can result in hidden-dimension-free (up to logarithmic factors) sublinear regret with respect to the best LDS predictor.

\paragraph{Does this transfer to zero-shot time-series forecasting?} We investigate how the USP framework can be applied to pretrained time series foundation models, and specifically to the Moirai 2.0 model. The setting of pretrained time series foundation models~\citep{woo2024unified,liu2024moirai2,das2024decoder,ansari2024chronos,goswami2024moment,auer2025tirex} differs from USP in two important ways. First, the forecaster is trained offline on a large corpus and deployed for zero-shot prediction over a multi-step horizon, rather than making one-step predictions in an online loop where the true observation is revealed after each step. Second, the training data comes from many heterogeneous sources that may not be well-modelled as finite-dimensional LDSs.

\paragraph{Our approach.}
A direct way to use USP would be to train the forecaster to predict the preconditioned target sequence and
then invert the preconditioning filter to recover forecasts in the original scale. For multi-step
forecasting, this inversion may lead to error accumulation as each recovered future value depends on earlier recovered
values, so forecast errors are fed back into later predictions. To address this we can leave the forecast target unchanged and concatenate the preconditioned signal as an auxiliary input channel alongside the raw target and the observation mask. Because the model never operates in preconditioned space at inference, no inversion is needed and error accumulation is avoided.

A second motivation comes from the architecture of Moirai 2.0 itself. Patch-based transformers~\citep{nie2023patchtst,dosovitskiy2021image} split the input into non-overlapping length-$P$ windows and embed each patch independently, creating a cross-patch information bottleneck: before the first self-attention layer, each patch embedding encodes only $P$ time steps, so multi-patch correlations must be discovered entirely through attention. A polynomial preconditioner that mixes information across the same multi-patch scales before patching can inject cross-patch structure into every embedding at negligible parameter cost.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(1)~We introduce polynomial input preconditioning, a simple modification adding a fixed Chebyshev FIR-filtered channel to patch-based time series transformers.
(2)~Through 5-seed experiments on 97 GIFT-Eval configurations, we show a $2.9\%$ improvement ($p < 10^{-15}$) and control for confounds: a duplicate channel hurts, a zero channel is neutral. At 100K steps, the benefit grows to $3.9\%$.
(3)~Ablations identify degree 4, patch-aligned stride, Chebyshev basis, and 10\% hint dropout as optimal.
(4)~Domain, frequency, and horizon breakdowns show the benefit is strongest where the filter's lookback scale aligns with structured temporal patterns.
(5)~The result is confirmed on the independent FEV-Bench benchmark (100 tasks, $-2.2\%$ MASE, 78/100 wins).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Time Series Foundation Models}

Recent work trains large models on diverse time series corpora for zero-shot forecasting. Decoder-only approaches include TimesFM~\citep{das2024decoder} and Lag-Llama~\citep{rasul2024lagllama}. Tokenization-based methods include Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos}, which trains a T5 model on discretized values. Masked encoder approaches include Moirai~\citep{woo2024unified} with any-variate attention and frequency-adaptive patching, Moirai~2.0~\citep{liu2024moirai2} which introduces a decoder-only architecture, MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment}, and TTM~\citep{ekambaram2024ttm}. TimeGPT~\citep{garza2024timegpt} and Timer~\citep{liu2024timer} use generative pre-training, while UniTS~\citep{gao2024units} unifies multiple tasks. Several surveys~\citep{liang2024foundation,yuqi2024survey} and critical evaluations~\citep{zhang2025tsfmready} discuss the landscape. We build on Moirai~2.0 Small (11.4M parameters), which uses the LOTSA corpus~\citep{woo2024unified} containing 27B observations across 9 domains.

\subsection{Patching and Cross-Patch Information}

PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst} introduced channel-independent patching for time series, splitting the input into non-overlapping windows of size $P$ and projecting each independently before transformer processing. This approach was adopted by Moirai~\citep{woo2024unified} and TimesFM~\citep{das2024decoder}. The patch-level tokenization introduces an information bottleneck: each initial embedding is blind to temporal patterns spanning multiple patches. PatchTST partially addresses this through optional patch overlap, and \citet{han2024capacity} analyze the capacity--robustness tradeoff of channel-independent processing. Alternatives that address cross-variate or cross-patch interactions include iTransformer~\citep{liu2024itransformer} (variate-level attention), Crossformer~\citep{zhang2023crossformer} (cross-dimension attention), and multi-patch prediction~\citep{chen2024multi}. Our approach is orthogonal: rather than modifying the attention mechanism or patching strategy, we inject cross-patch information at the input level through a fixed polynomial filter, requiring no changes to the transformer stack.

\subsection{Spectral Methods in Sequence Modelling}

Spectral approaches to sequence modelling have a rich history. \citet{hazan2017learning} introduced spectral filtering for learning linear dynamical systems, extended by \citet{hazan2018spectral} to general LDS. The recently proposed spectral state space models~\citep{agarwal2025spectral} and Flash STU~\citep{dao2024flashstu} leverage polynomial structure for efficient sequence processing. In time series specifically, FEDformer~\citep{zhou2022fedformer} uses frequency-enhanced decomposition, FiLM~\citep{zhou2022film} applies Legendre polynomial projections for long-term memory, and FreTS~\citep{yi2024frequency} operates entirely in the frequency domain. Structured state space models~\citep{gu2022efficiently} and their selective variant Mamba~\citep{gu2024mamba} also use polynomial-like parameterizations. Our work bridges the gap between the theoretical USP framework~\citep{marsden2024universal} and practical patch-based forecasters, showing that a fixed polynomial FIR filter applied before tokenization provides a consistent benefit.

\subsection{Input Preprocessing for Time Series}

Input transformations are a well-established technique for improving time series models. Reversible instance normalization (RevIN)~\citep{kim2022reversible} applies learnable affine normalization to handle distribution shift. DAIN~\citep{passalis2020deep} uses adaptive input normalization. Classical differencing~\citep{box1970time} removes trends and non-stationarity. WaveNet~\citep{oord2016wavenet} uses dilated causal convolutions to capture multi-scale temporal patterns. Our polynomial preconditioning can be seen as a generalization of first-order differencing: a degree-$d$ Chebyshev polynomial at stride $s$ provides a principled multi-scale lookback that captures structure at $2s, 4s, \ldots, ds$ lags, with coefficients derived from approximation theory rather than learned.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Background: Patch-Based Time Series Transformers}

Consider a univariate time series $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_T)$ with observation mask $m$. Following Moirai~\citep{woo2024unified,liu2024moirai2}, the model applies instance normalization, then partitions the series into non-overlapping patches of size $P$: $\mathbf{p}_i = (x_{(i-1)P+1}, \ldots, x_{iP})$. Each patch and its mask form a $2P$-dimensional input projected to $d_{\text{model}}$ dimensions via a residual block:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{e}_i = \text{ResBlock}_{2P \to d}([\mathbf{p}_i; \mathbf{m}_i]).
\end{equation}

Moirai 2.0 Small uses a 6-layer decoder-only transformer with $d_{\mathrm{model}} = 384$, $d_{\mathrm{ff}} = 1024$, 6 attention heads, causal multi-head self-attention with rotary position embeddings (RoPE), SwiGLU feed-forward blocks, and RMSNorm. The output projection is a ResBlock mapping $\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^{K \times |\mathcal{Q}| \times P}$ where $K{=}4$ is the multi-token horizon and $|\mathcal{Q}|{=}9$ the number of quantile levels.

The key observation is that before the first self-attention layer, each patch embedding encodes only $P$ consecutive time steps. Temporal patterns spanning multiple patches---daily cycles in hourly data ($24$ steps $= 1.5$ patches at $P=16$), weekly seasonality in 15-minute data ($672$ steps $= 42$ patches)---are invisible to individual embeddings and must be recovered entirely through attention.

\subsection{Preconditioning Polynomial}

Given a normalized input series $x$, let $\tilde T_d(z)=z^d+c_1 z^{d-1}+\cdots+c_d$ be the degree-$d$ monic Chebyshev polynomial in descending powers. Following the implementation convention, the leading monic term is the current value and the remaining coefficients are applied at stride-$s$ lags:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
r_t &= x_t^{\mathrm{pre}} - x_t
     = \sum_{k=1}^{d} c_k\,x_{t-ks}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:precond}
\end{equation}
where $s$ is a stride parameter set equal to the patch size $P$. Our default is the monic Chebyshev polynomial of the first kind, $\tilde T_d$, with $d=4$. For $P=16$ the coefficients are $(c_1,\dots,c_4) = (0,-1,0,\tfrac{1}{8})$, so $r_t = -x_{t-32} + \tfrac{1}{8}\, x_{t-64}$.

The choice of Chebyshev polynomials is motivated by their optimality in minimax polynomial approximation~\citep{mason2003chebyshev} and their well-conditioned spectral properties in the context of spectral filtering for LDS prediction~\citep{hazan2017learning,marsden2024universal}.

\subsection{Preconditioning Channel}

The residual $r$ is partitioned into patches $\mathbf{r}_i$ aligned with the data patches $\mathbf{p}_i$. In Moirai 2.0, each patch passes through a per-patch input projection (a two-layer residual MLP with SiLU activation) that maps the concatenation of the data patch and an observation mask to an embedding vector. We widen this projection to accept a third input, the preconditioning patch:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{e}_i = \text{ResBlock}_{3P \to d}([\mathbf{p}_i; \mathbf{m}_i; \mathbf{r}_i]).
\end{equation}

The input projection ResBlock consists of:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{z} &= \text{SiLU}(W_1 \mathbf{x} + b_1), & W_1 &\in \mathbb{R}^{d \times 3P}, \\
\mathbf{o} &= W_2 \mathbf{z} + b_2 + W_3 \mathbf{x} + b_3, & W_2 &\in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}, \; W_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times 3P}.
\end{align}
Only $W_1$ and $W_3$ are wider than the baseline. $W_2$ and all downstream layers are identical. The input dimension grows from $2P$ to $3P$, adding $12$K parameters ($0.11\%$ of Moirai 2.0 Small). The transformer decoder is unchanged.

\subsection{Channel Dropout}

During training with channel dropout probability $p$, each patch's preconditioning channel is independently zeroed with probability $p$, preventing overdependence on the preconditioning signal. At inference, the full preconditioning channel is always provided. We refer to the default configuration ($d=4$ Chebyshev, $s=16$, no dropout) as \texttt{d4}, and to the same architecture trained with $10\%$ per-patch channel dropout as \texttt{d4\_dropout}.

The $z$-score normalization that precedes the residual computation uses only observed context values; prediction-window positions are excluded, so no future information leaks into $r_t$. Unobserved time steps are masked to zero.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

We train Moirai 2.0 Small \citep{liu2024moirai2} (11.4M params, 6-layer transformer, $P{=}16$) from scratch on the public LOTSA corpus \citep{woo2024unified} for 10K steps (batch 256, AdamW, cosine LR $10^{-3}$, 1K warmup, bf16). The official Moirai 2.0 model was trained for 100K steps on 295B observations, while our accessible LOTSA setup contains 27B observations and is run for either 10K or 100K steps. All comparisons in this paper use identical data, optimizer, loss, precision, and seeds within the compared conditions. The complete training protocol is detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:methodology}.

We then train models \texttt{d4} and \texttt{d4\_dropout} with the architectural modifications described above. For \texttt{d4\_dropout}, the preconditioning channel is independently zeroed with probability $0.1$ per patch during training and always provided at inference. All trained models share identical data, optimiser, seeds, and compute, so the effects we report are controlled relative improvements.

\subsection{Evaluation}

At inference, the model predicts multiple future patches per step. For longer horizons, we proceed with autoregressive decoding, where predicted patches are appended to the context and the decoder is re-run with the preconditioning residual recomputed over the extended context.

Evaluation uses GIFT-Eval \citep{vijay2024gifteval} (97 dataset$\times$horizon configs) at context length 4{,}000 and the independent FEV-Bench \citep{shchur2025fevbench} (100 tasks). GIFT-Eval is a large-scale zero-shot benchmark for general and foundation time-series forecasters, spanning 23 datasets, over 144{,}000 time series, seven domains, ten sampling frequencies, multivariate inputs, and short- to long-horizon tasks. FEV-Bench is a realistic forecasting benchmark for reproducible and statistically sound model comparison, comprising 100 tasks from 96 datasets across seven real-world domains, including covariate-rich univariate and multivariate settings. We report normalized MASE following the GIFT-Eval leaderboard convention: each configuration's MASE is divided by the seasonal naive baseline's MASE, then aggregated via geometric mean. We run 5 seeds (0, 1, 2, 7, 42) and report cross-seed mean and standard deviation.

\subsection{Conditions}

We compare four conditions, all sharing the same training data, optimizer, and seeds. \textbf{Baseline} is unmodified Moirai 2.0 (no preconditioning channel). \textbf{\texttt{d4}} and \textbf{\texttt{d4\_dropout}} add the polynomial preconditioning channel of \S\ref{sec:method} (without and with $10\%$ per-patch channel dropout). \textbf{Zero} and \textbf{Duplicate} are capacity-matched controls: they share \texttt{d4\_dropout}'s widened input projection and $12$K extra parameters, but feed the third channel either all zeros (Zero) or a copy of the data channel (Duplicate). This helps us determine whether changes in performance are due to the polynomial content or just from the extra capacity.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Domain and Frequency Analysis}

The benefit is heterogeneous across domains. The strongest improvements appear in transport ($+6.9\%$, $p = 4 \times 10^{-8}$), energy ($+4.0\%$, $p = 2 \times 10^{-4}$), and demographics ($+9.8\%$, $p = 0.004$), while weather ($+0.3\%$, $p = 0.68$) and healthcare ($+0.2\%$, $p = 0.62$) are neutral. By frequency, the benefit is strongest at sub-hourly ($+3.4\%$) and hourly ($+3.8\%$) frequencies. This is consistent with the mechanism: at stride $s{=}16$ the filter looks back 32 and 64 steps---for 15-minute data, this captures intra-day patterns driving transport and energy demand, while for daily weather data the lookback spans 32--64 days, too long for relevant patterns. The benefit also increases with prediction horizon: $+1.4\%$ for short horizons to $+5.3\%$ for long horizons. Detailed domain and frequency tables are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:domain}.

\subsection{Mechanism: Cross-Patch Information Leakage}

The preconditioning channel provides each patch embedding with information about \emph{neighbouring patches} at the input level. For $d{=}4$, $s{=}16$: $r_t = -x_{t-32} + \tfrac{1}{8}\, x_{t-64}$, i.e., the preconditioning residual for patch $i$ contains values from patches $i{-}2$ and $i{-}4$. This is conceptually related to overlapping patches in PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, but more efficient: instead of increasing sequence length, we inject cross-patch information through a separate channel with zero overhead in the transformer encoder. The optimality of stride $s = P$ supports this interpretation---when filter taps align with patch boundaries, they provide maximally informative cross-patch signal.

\subsection{Learning Rate Interaction}

\texttt{d4\_dropout}'s MASE is nearly identical at $\eta = 10^{-3}$ (0.8368) and $2 \times 10^{-3}$ (0.8361), a $0.09\%$ difference, while the baseline varies by $2.27\%$. This suggests two benefits: (1)~\emph{optimization stability}---the polynomial structure acts as an implicit regularizer smoothing the loss landscape; (2)~\emph{genuine inductive bias}---at matched optimal LR, the polynomial content still provides $0.7\%$ improvement ($p < 10^{-3}$). Across all 5 seeds, the training loss gap is consistently small relative to the eval gap. The matched learning rate comparison is detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:matchedlr}.

%==============================================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
%==============================================================================

We identify several limitations of our approach:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Optimization stability vs.\ inductive bias.} The improvement at default LR ($-2.9\%$) is larger than at matched optimal LR ($-0.7\%$). This means a substantial portion of the benefit comes from optimization stability (smoothing the loss landscape) rather than a pure inductive bias effect, though the residual improvement at matched LR remains statistically significant ($p < 10^{-3}$).

    \item \textbf{Domain-dependent benefit.} Weather and healthcare domains see no significant improvement, likely because the filter's lookback scale (32--64 time steps at stride 16) does not align with the relevant temporal patterns in these domains. Adapting the stride or degree per-domain could address this but would sacrifice the simplicity of a fixed, universal configuration.

    \item \textbf{Scale-dependent benefit.} Our experiments use Moirai~2.0 Small (11.4M parameters). Preliminary results on the Base model (46M parameters) are inconsistent across seeds (Appendix~\ref{app:base}), suggesting the benefit may be specific to capacity-constrained models where the cross-patch information bottleneck is more binding.

    \item \textbf{Fixed coefficients.} Fixed Chebyshev coefficients slightly outperform learned FIR filters (\S\ref{sec:ablations}), suggesting the polynomial structure is already near-optimal for this setting. However, this means the approach has limited room for task-specific adaptation.

    \item \textbf{Training data gap.} Our retraining uses 27B observations (public LOTSA) versus 295B in the official Moirai~2.0 release. While our controlled comparisons are valid (all conditions use the same data), the absolute performance gap to the official model suggests our findings should be verified at full data scale.
\end{enumerate}

%==============================================================================
\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================================

In this paper, we adapt the ideas presented in \citet{marsden2024universal} to zero-shot patch-based time-series transformers. Concatenating the preconditioned signal as an auxiliary channel avoids the error accumulation of target-side preconditioning at multi-step horizons, and adds only $0.11\%$ parameters, while providing a significant $2.9\%$ improvement on GIFT-Eval. At a 100K-step budget, the method remains substantially better than the matched baseline, with a larger relative gap and lower cross-seed variance. The improvement is also largest at longer horizon lengths.

More broadly, these results suggest that time-series foundation models can still benefit substantially from simple, theory-guided input transformations. A fixed polynomial channel derived from USP theory improves zero-shot forecasting across benchmarks without significantly increasing parameter count or changing the transformer stack. This points to a practical path for making compact forecasters more accurate and parameter-efficient by injecting structure before tokenization, rather than relying only on larger models or heavier attention mechanisms.

The same principle may extend beyond time series. Pretrained language models and other sequence models could benefit from lightweight, fixed preconditioning channels that expose long-range structure before tokenization or early embedding layers, offering a complementary alternative to scaling model size alone.

\subsection{Preconditioning Channel Implementation}

The monic Chebyshev polynomial $\tilde T_d(z)=z^d+c_1 z^{d-1}+\cdots+c_d$ is expanded in the monomial basis to obtain the coefficients used by the preconditioning filter. The preconditioning residual for time step $t$ is:
\[
r_t = \left(x_t + \sum_{k=1}^{d} c_k \, x_{t-ks}\right) - x_t
\]
This is computed over the full (normalized, zero-mean unit-variance) time series before patching. Unobserved time steps are masked to zero. During training with channel dropout $p$, each patch's preconditioning channel independently has probability $p$ of being replaced with zeros. At inference, the full preconditioning channel is always provided.

The preconditioning channel is concatenated with the target and mask channels before the input projection:
\[
\mathbf{e}_i = \text{ResBlock}_{3P \to d}([\mathbf{p}_i; \mathbf{m}_i; \mathbf{r}_i])
\]

\section{Matched-Official Protocol (100K Steps, 10K Warmup)}
\label{app:matched_official}
%==============================================================================

The 100K results in the main paper use a 1,000-step warmup, while the official Moirai~2.0 training protocol~\citep{liu2024moirai2} uses 10,000 steps of warmup. To control for this protocol difference, we retrain both baseline and \texttt{d4\_dropout} at 100K steps with the exact official hyperparameters.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Matched-official 100K results (10K warmup, cosine annealing to 0, otherwise identical to official protocol).}
\label{tab:matched_official}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{s0} & \textbf{s2} & \textbf{s7} & \textbf{Mean} & \textbf{vs BL} \\
\midrule
Baseline (1K warmup) & 0.909 & 0.863 & 0.864 & 0.878 & --- \\
Baseline (10K warmup) & 0.882 & 0.880 & 0.917 & 0.893 & --- \\
\midrule
\texttt{d4\_dropout} (1K warmup) & 0.835 & 0.859 & 0.845 & 0.844 & $-3.9\%$ \\
\texttt{d4\_dropout} (10K warmup) & 0.892 & 0.846 & 0.880 & \textbf{0.873} & $\mathbf{-2.3\%}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

With 10K warmup (matching the official protocol), \texttt{d4\_dropout} still outperforms the baseline by $2.3\%$ on average across 3 seeds (compared to $3.9\%$ with 1K warmup). The smaller advantage reflects that the longer warmup partially stabilizes the baseline---at seed 0, BL improves from 0.909 (1K warmup, which shows the late-training collapse) to 0.882 (10K warmup). The benefit is seed-dependent: \texttt{d4\_dropout} wins at seed 2 ($+3.8\%$) and seed 7 ($+4.0\%$), but slightly loses at seed 0 ($-1.1\%$). This suggests that part of \texttt{d4\_dropout}'s 100K advantage at 1K warmup is robustness to schedule-induced instability, but a genuine $\sim$2\% inductive-bias effect persists under the matched-official protocol.
